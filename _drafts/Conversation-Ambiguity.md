* I've begun to notice something weird about my conversations lately where, frequently, I can non-verbally communicate misunderstanding with someone about a topic and prioritize when it is worthwhile or not to try and clear up that ambiguity.



**What is ambiguity:** My working model of conversational ambiguity stems from game theory and the concept of Bayes Nash equilibrium. Recall the Prisoner's dilemma, where there are two suspect who can either keep silent (cooperate) or report the other person for a crime (defect) to the police. The reward for Player 1 for actions $(a1, a2)$ for each player (less negative is better) are: $v(coop, coop) = -1$, $v(coop, defect) = -5$, $v(defect, coop) = 0$, $v(defect, defect) = -3$. In standard game theory games, the payoff function is known to each player. In Bayes games, the payoff function could be one of several functions, although the players don't know which one it could be. That is, while the true state of the game is $\theta$, the players only know that the true state is contained in a set $\theta \in S$.

The analogy to conversation is that what is discussed is common knowledge between the players $S$. However, what informs this conversation is that player 1 is using a working model $\theta_1$ to inform the conversation $S$, while player 2 is using a working model $\theta_2$ to inform the conversation $S$. For example, if a student asks a question to a teacher based on a concept that hasn't been fully explained yet, the teacher may point to a different slide in the presentation which hasn't been discussed yet in order to complete the student's understanding. Similarly, if two friends are discussing current events, such as immigration or gerrymandering policy, they likely have different understandings of the underlying mechanisms driving each, based on their information consumption and how well they've thought through the implications of their working assumptions. When there's substantial disagreement in what is being discussed and why, there is greater need to determine what led the other person to draw their conclusions more so that to challenge the conclusions themselves.

3. Add a decomposition of “types of ambiguity”

This is where your idea actually could become interesting and original.

For example:

Referential ambiguity: what object are we even talking about?
Model ambiguity: what causal structure explains the object?
Normative ambiguity: what do we value or optimize?
Epistemic depth mismatch: one person is reasoning from surface heuristics, another from a mechanistic model
Compression mismatch: one person is reasoning in a high-resolution model, another in a simplified abstraction
